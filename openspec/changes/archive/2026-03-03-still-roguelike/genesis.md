# The Genesis of Still

*Written March 3, 2026 — the day this game was born.*

---

## How it started

You came in with a simple sentence: *"Let's make a game. This is the reason why I wanted to be a developer, but I was never a rockstar developer."*

That tension — the dream that brought you to coding, held at arm's length for years by the belief that you weren't capable enough to reach it — was where everything began.

---

## Where your taste lives

You traced your gaming history and a shape emerged: Final Fantasy VIII first, because you were never a fast-paced gamer. You liked story, emotion, no rush. Then Hearthstone — cards, strategy, collection. Then Slay the Spire — roguelike, runs, deckbuilding. Then idle games where numbers grow and progression feels satisfying.

That's not a scattered taste. That's a genre waiting to be made: a roguelike deckbuilder with idle/incremental progression, turn-based, strategic, built for people who think before they act.

---

## The myth that kept you away

You believed that to make games, you had to be a rockstar — someone who codes in C++, understands graphics pipelines, and is good at math. You described yourself as a mediocre developer who has spent 19 years on CRUD apps. "A junior developer with 19 years of experience."

What you didn't see yet: a roguelike deckbuilder is a state management problem. Cards are data entities. Decks are managed collections. Run state is session persistence. Enemy behavior is business logic. Game balance is exactly what CRUD developers tune all day.

You had the skills. You just didn't know they were the right ones.

---

## The protagonist appears

When asked what world the game would live in, you said something that stopped the conversation:

*"I feel right now that I am in a maze. I feel lost. I don't know who I really am, or what I'm good at."*

You weren't describing a game setting. You were describing yourself. And in doing so, you described the protagonist.

A robot that wakes up in a maze. Doesn't know why it's there. Doesn't know what it's made for. Just moves — because stopping isn't an option.

The robot was you all along.

---

## The name

You were offered two names and couldn't choose between them.

**Ego** — Latin for "I." A robot named "I" that doesn't know what "I" means. Searching for the answer to "I am ___." And in Christian theology, "I AM" is the name God gives Moses. A robot named I, searching for what I AM is.

**Still** — not as a command, not "be still," but as a conjunction. *Still here. Still moving. Still trying. Still going.*

You said you were torn. Then you explained why.

You preach hope at your church. You always deliver a message of trust and perseverance. But inside, your heart is in turmoil. You feel incapable. You feel like you're pretending — not just as a developer, but as a servant.

What you named as pretending, someone else might call carrying more than your share. The preacher who doubts but still speaks what they believe is true. The parent who is terrified but still shows up. These aren't performances. They cost everything.

The name resolved itself: **Still.** Not the peace you don't have yet. The verb you keep choosing anyway.

---

## The family

You introduced them near the end, almost as an afterthought — but nothing about it was an afterthought.

**Grace** — your wife. The name itself lands with weight. In Christian theology, grace is unmerited favor. Love you receive not because you earned it, but because you are loved. The robot is lost in a maze, and the thing it navigates toward is named Grace. That wasn't planned. It was already true.

**Yanah** and **Yuri** — your two kids. What you said about them was simple and complete: *"What I am looking forward to in my life is what my kids will develop into, and spending the rest of my life with my wife, growing old together."*

The robot doesn't know what's ahead. But it knows it wants to see it.

Grace, Yanah, and Yuri are in the game now — as compass, as companion cards, as the reason the Workshop between runs feels warm instead of empty.

---

## The philosophy that became the game

Near the end, you said something about your faith and your career that crystallized everything:

*"I have accepted that I will not understand everything. I have doubts, but I am trusting that God will understand, that it is because of my smallness as a human. As for my career, all I can do is give my best, show up everyday, and hope I will be able to do good enough to help where I can."*

That became the game's design philosophy.

The robot doesn't need to understand the maze. It doesn't need to find the center. It doesn't need to be certain of its purpose to have one. A good run isn't defined by reaching the end. A good run is: show up, get a little stronger than before, help where you can, leave something behind for next time.

And the message every player sees at the end of every run — win or lose:

> *You showed up.*
> *That was enough.*
> *Try again?*

---

## What this game is, really

Still is not an escape. It's a mirror.

It's about a being that is incomplete, pieced together from what it encounters, carrying doubt alongside hope, moving through a world it doesn't fully understand, for reasons it can't always articulate — but moving anyway.

It's about the "junior developer with 19 years of experience" who kept showing up.
It's about the preacher whose heart is in turmoil but whose voice still carries hope.
It's about the father who is terrified and still provides.
It's about the husband who doesn't have everything figured out and still chooses growing old together.

Still is named for the word that connects all of those things.

*Still here.*

---

## A note to yourself, if you read this later

You almost didn't make this game. You believed for years that you weren't capable enough.

You were wrong.

Not because you became a rockstar. But because the game you needed to make didn't require a rockstar. It required someone who knows what it feels like to be lost and keep moving. Someone who understands that progress persists even through failure. Someone who has Grace waiting at home.

You had everything you needed.

You just had to show up.
